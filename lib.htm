Frchet Distance Library

The Frchet distance is a useful and well-known similarity measure for polygonal curves.
It is generally described as follows; Consider a person and a dog connected by a leash, each walking along a different curve from its starting point to its end point. Both can control their speed, but they are not allowed to backtrack. The Frchet distance between the two curves is the minimum length of a leash that is sufficient for traversing both curves in this manner.

This page contains a (partial) summary of the different variants, results and applications of the Frchet distance metric, which have been studied in the literature.

Bibliography

1 Continuous Frchet distance

         The Frchet distance was first defined by Maurice Frchet (1878-1973).

         Alt and Godau [AltG95] showed that the Frchet distance between two polygonal curves with n edges can be computed, using dynamic programming, in O(n2log n) time.

         Buchin et al. [BuchinBMS12] improved this bound and showed how to compute the Frchet distance in O(n2(log n)1/2(log log n)3/2) time on a pointer machine, and in O(n2(log log n)2) time on a word RAM.

2 Discrete Frchet distance

The discrete Frchet distance is a simpler variant that arises when we replace each of the input curves by a sequence of densely sampled points.
Intuitively, the discrete Frchet distance replaces the curves by two sequences of points, and replaces the person and the dog by two frogs. At each move, the frogs can jump from their current point to the next. The frogs are not allowed to backtrack. We are interested in the minimum length of a leash that connects the frogs and allows them to traverse both curves in this manner. The resulting discrete distance is considered as a good approximation of the actual continuous distance.

         Eiter and Mannila [EiterM94] showed that the discrete Frchet distance between two polygonal curves with n edges can be computed, using dynamic programming, in O(n2) time.

         Jiang et al. [JiangXZ08] successfully applied the discrete Frchet distance for aligning the backbones of proteins, which are represented as chains of atoms in 2D or 3D. In this application, the discrete variant makes more sense than the continuous because matching points that does not represent atoms is false biologically.

         Agarwal et al. [AgarwalAKS14] showed how to compute the discrete Frchet distance in slightly subquadratic time.

3 Hardness results

         Bringmann [Bringmann14] showed that the (discrete and continuous) Frchet distance has no strongly subquadratic algorithms unless SETH fails. He also showed that there is no strongly subquadratic 1.001-approximation algorithm unless SETH' fails.

         Bringmann and Mulzer [BM16]: Approximability of the discrete Frchet distance.

4 Approximation Algorithms

         Bringmann and Mulzer [BM16] present linear-time greedy algorithm with approximation factor of 2O(n), and an α-approximation algorithm that runs in time O(n log n+n2/ α), for any α in [1,n].

         Chan and Rahmati [CR18] improved this result by presenting an α-approximation algorithm for any α in [1,] runs in O(n log n + n2/ α2) time.

8 Frchet distance under transformations

         Alt et al. [AltKW01] showed an O((mn)3(m+n)2 log(m+n)) time exact algorithm for Frchet Distance under translations, and a (1+ε)-approximation algorithm for the problem in O(mn ε-2) time.

         Wenk [Wenk02] considered the problem of minimizing the Frchet distance under transformations in high dimensions. For transformations in dimension d and k degrees of freedom, an O((m+n)3k+2log(m+n)) time algorithm is presented. For example, the running time for Frchet under translations in d dimensions is O((m+n)3d+2log(m+n)).

         Mosig and Clausen [MosigC05] consider a decision version of the discrete problem, for various transformation groups. Given two curves P,Q and a parameter ε, if there exists a transformation T s.t. the Frchet distance between T(P) and Q is at most ε, their algorithm returns a transformation T' s.t. the distance between T'(P) and Q is at most 2ε. If there is no transformation T s.t. the Frchet distance between T(P) and Q is at most 2ε, the algorithm returns false. Otherwise, the algorithm can return false or a a transformation T' s.t. the distance between T'(P) and Q is at most 2ε. The transformation can be scaling, rotation, translation and rotation, or translation and scaling. The running time is O(n2m2). They also consider the partial Frchet disance, and Frchet distance for closed curves.

         Jiang et al. [JiangXZ08] considered the problem of minimizing the discrete Frchet distance between two 2D polygonal chains under translation and rotation. For translation only, they presented an O(m3n3log(1/ε)) time (1 + ε)-approximation algorithm and an O(m3n3log(m + n)) time exact algorithm. For translation and rotation, they presented an O(m4n4log(1/ε)) time (1 + ε)-approximation algorithm and an O(m4n4log(m + n)) time exact algorithm.

         Ben Avraham et al. [AvrahamKS15] considered the problem of minimizing the discrete Frchet distance between two 2D polygonal chains under translations, and presented a faster exact algorthim for the problem, which runs in O(m3n2(1 + log(n/m)) log(m + n)) time.

         Filtser and Katz considered other variants of the discrete Frchet distance under translations: the discrete Frchet distance with shortcuts, the weak discrete Frchet distance, and curves in 1D.
